#### 1. Which state agency is the primary resource for native plant community classification, scientific and natural areas, and invasive species management in Minnesota?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Minnesota Department of Transportation (MnDOT)
2. Minnesota Pollution Control Agency (MPCA)
3. Minnesota Department of Natural Resources (DNR)
4. Minnesota Department of Agriculture (MDA)
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **C**. The Minnesota Department of Natural Resources (DNR) is the state's primary agency for managing fish, wildlife, forests, and native plant communities. The DNR maintains the most comprehensive native plant community classification, oversees 160+ Scientific and Natural Areas, coordinates invasive species programs, issues seed harvest permits, and provides prescribed burn assistance. For native plant enthusiasts, the DNR is the most important state-level resource.

#### 3. What does Minnesota's Buffer Law require?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. All homes must have a 50-foot setback from the nearest road
2. Perennial vegetative buffers of native or approved species must be maintained along public waterways to protect water quality
3. All farm fields must be surrounded by stone walls
4. Native plants cannot be planted within 100 feet of any lake
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Minnesota's Buffer Law (passed in 2015) requires perennial vegetative buffers along public waters and public ditches. These buffers, planted with native or approved species, intercept runoff, filter pollutants, reduce erosion, and provide habitat corridors for wildlife. The law recognizes the essential role that shoreline and riparian vegetation plays in protecting Minnesota's water quality and reflects the same ecological principles covered in the wetland and shoreline chapters.

#### 5. Under Minnesota's Noxious Weed Law, what is the difference between "Prohibited Eradicate" and "Prohibited Control" species?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. There is no practical difference between the categories
2. Prohibited Eradicate species must be destroyed wherever found; Prohibited Control species must be managed to prevent spread
3. Prohibited Eradicate applies to plants; Prohibited Control applies to insects
4. Prohibited Eradicate species are protected from removal; Prohibited Control species may be voluntarily removed
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Minnesota's Noxious Weed Law categorizes harmful species by required action: Prohibited Eradicate species (like Palmer amaranth) must be destroyed wherever found, while Prohibited Control species (like Canada thistle and purple loosestrife) must be managed to prevent spread. Understanding these categories matters for native plant gardeners because some municipalities have used weed ordinances to mistakenly target tall native plantings, so knowing the law helps protect legitimate native gardens.

    **Concept Tested:** MN Noxious Weed Law

    The correct answer is **B**. The most effective approach starts with your county SWCD, which provides free technical advice and connects you to cost-share programs. This sequence maximizes your resources and ensures your project meets program requirements.

#### 7. What is the RIM Reserve program, and what does it accomplish for native plant conservation?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. A museum program that displays preserved native plant specimens
2. A permanent conservation easement program that pays landowners to retire marginal agricultural land and restore it to native vegetation
3. A temporary rental program for urban community garden plots
4. A program that reserves native plant seeds for future scientific research
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. The Reinvest in Minnesota (RIM) Reserve program, administered by BWSR, is a permanent conservation easement program. It pays landowners to permanently retire marginal agricultural land and restore it to native vegetation, particularly around wetlands and waterways. RIM easements protect restored native plantings in perpetuity, making them one of the most durable tools for native plant conservation and water quality protection in the state.

    **Concept Tested:** BWSR Programs

</div>

Many cities are updating their ordinances to be more native-plant-friendly, but checking first prevents conflicts.

#### 10. Evaluate this scenario: A Minnesota landowner receives a flyer from a company offering to install a "native prairie" using a generic seed mix from out of state. What concerns should the landowner consider?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. No concerns; any seed mix labeled "native" will work in Minnesota
2. The seed mix may lack local ecotype genetics, contain inappropriate species for the site's ecoregion, or include nativars with reduced ecological value
3. Out-of-state seed mixes are illegal in Minnesota
4. Generic seed mixes always perform better than locally sourced mixes
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Generic out-of-state seed mixes raise several concerns: they may lack local ecotype genetics adapted to Minnesota's specific climate and growing season, contain species that are native to other regions but not well-suited to the landowner's ecoregion, or include nativars with reduced ecological value. The landowner should ask about seed provenance, request a species list matched to their site conditions, and consider consulting their county SWCD or a reputable Minnesota native plant nursery for locally appropriate alternatives.
