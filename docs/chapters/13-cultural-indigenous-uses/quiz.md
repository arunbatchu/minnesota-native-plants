#### 1. What is ethnobotany?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. The study of how to grow plants in ethnic restaurants
2. The scientific study of relationships between people and plants, including how different cultures identify, name, and use plants
3. A branch of medicine that uses only plant-based treatments
4. The study of plant genetics and breeding
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Ethnobotany is the scientific study of relationships between people and plants. It documents how different cultures identify, name, classify, and use plants for food, medicine, shelter, tools, dyes, ceremony, and more. The term combines "ethno" (relating to cultures and peoples) and "botany" (the study of plants). In Minnesota, ethnobotany includes the rich traditions of the Dakota and Ojibwe peoples, who have held vast plant knowledge for millennia.

#### 2. How does Indigenous plant knowledge differ from Western scientific approaches to understanding plants?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Indigenous knowledge is outdated and has been replaced by modern science
2. Indigenous knowledge is relational, place-based, and holistic, seeing plants as living relatives; Western science tends to isolate chemical compounds and study parts in isolation
3. There is no meaningful difference between the two approaches
4. Western science is based on observation while Indigenous knowledge is purely spiritual
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Indigenous plant knowledge is relational (understanding plants through relationships, not just categories), place-based (tied to specific landscapes and seasons), holistic (considering the whole plant, person, and community), and dynamic (continuing to evolve). Western science tends to ask what chemical compounds a plant contains, while Indigenous knowledge asks about the relationship and responsibilities involved. Both approaches have value, and neither is complete without the other.

#### 3. Why is intellectual sovereignty an important concept when learning about Indigenous plant knowledge?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. It means Indigenous peoples hold copyright on all plants in Minnesota
2. Indigenous plant knowledge belongs to the communities that hold it; not all knowledge is meant to be shared publicly, and some is sacred or restricted
3. It means only scientists can study Indigenous plant uses
4. It prevents anyone from growing native plants without tribal permission
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Intellectual sovereignty means that Indigenous plant knowledge belongs to the communities that hold it. Not all knowledge is meant to be shared publicly — some is sacred or restricted to certain people, clans, or ceremonies. Engaging with Indigenous knowledge requires humility, respect, and a willingness to listen. Readers are encouraged to seek out Indigenous-led educational programs rather than extracting knowledge from communities without permission.

#### 4. How did Indigenous peoples, particularly the Dakota, use fire to manage Minnesota's prairies?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. They burned prairies accidentally while cooking meals
2. They set strategic fires to encourage new grass growth, attract bison, improve travel routes, and maintain open landscapes
3. They used fire only to clear land for farming
4. Indigenous peoples avoided fire and tried to prevent all prairie burns
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Indigenous peoples, including the Dakota, managed the prairie with fire for millennia. They burned strategically to encourage new grass growth that attracted bison, to improve travel routes, and to maintain open landscapes. The prairie that European settlers encountered was not untouched wilderness — it was a landscape actively shaped by thousands of years of human stewardship. This traditional burning practice is a form of sophisticated land management now recognized as ecologically essential.

#### 5. Which of the following correctly describes Wild Rice (manoomin) in the context of Indigenous culture?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. It is a recently introduced crop that Indigenous peoples adopted from European settlers
2. It is a sacred food and cultural anchor for the Ojibwe and Dakota peoples, traditionally harvested from canoes using wooden knockers
3. It was used only for trade with European fur traders
4. It grows only in cultivated paddies managed with modern agricultural equipment
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Wild Rice (manoomin in Ojibwe) holds deep cultural and spiritual importance for the Ojibwe, Dakota, and other Indigenous peoples. Traditional harvesting involves two people in a canoe — one poling through rice beds while the other uses knockers (wooden sticks) to gently tap ripe grains free. This sustainable method leaves many seeds to reseed naturally. Wild Rice remains a staple food and anchor of cultural identity, not merely an economic commodity.

    **Concept Tested:** Food Plants Traditional

#### 6. What ethical principles should guide wild harvesting of native plants?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Take as much as you can carry since native plants are a free public resource
2. Harvest only from large healthy populations, take only what you need, leave enough for the plant to reproduce, and never harvest rare or threatened species
3. Wild harvesting is always prohibited regardless of species or location
4. Harvest only during full moon phases for maximum potency
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Ethical wild harvesting follows several principles: harvest only from large, healthy populations that can sustain the loss; take only what you need; leave enough for the plant to reproduce and for wildlife to use; never harvest rare, threatened, or endangered species; obtain permission from landowners; and follow Indigenous protocols of asking permission from the plant and offering thanks. Wild harvesting is a privilege that comes with responsibility to the plant, the ecosystem, and future generations.

#### 7. Paper Birch bark has been used by Indigenous peoples for centuries for practical purposes. What is the sustainable way to obtain birch bark?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Strip bark from the largest living birch trees for the best quality material
2. Harvest bark from fallen trees, because stripping bark from living trees damages and often kills them
3. There is no sustainable method; birch bark use should be discontinued
4. Use only commercially manufactured synthetic birch bark substitutes
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Birch bark can be sustainably harvested from fallen or dead trees. Stripping bark from living Paper Birch trees damages the tree's protective layer and often leads to infection and death. Indigenous peoples have long understood this distinction and traditionally harvested bark responsibly from downed trees and select branches. This sustainable practice exemplifies the respect for living organisms embedded in traditional ecological knowledge.

</div>

#### 9. What does "traditional land management" encompass beyond just farming or gardening?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Only the use of hand tools for vegetation clearing
2. A comprehensive approach including controlled burning, selective harvesting, transplanting, tending specific plant populations, and managing entire landscapes for ecological and cultural purposes
3. Leaving all land completely untouched by human activity
4. Applying modern agricultural chemicals to increase crop yields
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Traditional land management by Indigenous peoples encompassed a comprehensive suite of practices: controlled burning to maintain prairies and open woodlands, selective harvesting that sustained plant populations, transplanting and tending important species, and managing entire landscapes for both ecological health and cultural needs. This management shaped Minnesota's landscapes for thousands of years. The prairie and oak savanna ecosystems that European settlers encountered were products of active Indigenous stewardship, not untouched wilderness.

    **Concept Tested:** Traditional Land Mgmt

#### 10. Why is it important to recognize that Indigenous plant knowledge is a "living tradition" rather than a historical artifact?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Because Indigenous communities charge fees for sharing their knowledge
2. Because Indigenous knowledge continues to evolve as communities adapt, innovate, and reclaim traditional practices in the present day
3. Because all traditional practices must be frozen exactly as they were 500 years ago
4. Because living traditions require annual government renewal certificates
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Indigenous plant knowledge is not frozen in the past — it is a dynamic, living tradition that continues to evolve as communities adapt to changing conditions, innovate new approaches, and reclaim practices that were disrupted by colonization. Recognizing this means engaging with Indigenous communities as contemporary knowledge holders, not as relics of the past. It also means respecting that communities themselves decide how their knowledge is shared, taught, and applied.

    **Concept Tested:** Indigenous Plant Knowledge
