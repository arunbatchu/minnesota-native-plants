#### 1. What is the most common and costly mistake people make when cutting buckthorn?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Cutting in the wrong season
2. Using dull tools that damage the bark
3. Cutting without treating the stumps with herbicide, which causes vigorous resprouting of multiple new stems
4. Leaving the cut stems on the ground instead of chipping them
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **C**. If you cut buckthorn without immediately treating the stump with herbicide, the plant responds by sending up five to ten new stems from the cut stump, often growing back denser than before. This makes the infestation worse, not better. The most effective approach is to cut the stem and immediately apply herbicide (typically triclopyr or glyphosate) to the freshly cut stump surface before it begins to seal.

#### 2. How long should you plan to conduct follow-up monitoring and seedling removal after clearing a buckthorn-infested site?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. One growing season is sufficient
2. At least three to five years, because seeds persist in the soil seed bank
3. Follow-up is only needed if you did not use herbicide
4. Two weeks after the initial removal
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Buckthorn seeds can remain viable in the soil for five or more years, creating a persistent seed bank that produces new seedlings long after parent plants are removed. A heavily infested site may have hundreds of seeds per square meter. Planning for at least three to five years of follow-up seedling removal is essential. The seed bank is the primary reason that one-time removal efforts fail.

#### 3. What are the four main categories of invasive species removal methods?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Burning, flooding, freezing, and shading
2. Manual, mechanical, chemical, and biological
3. Cutting, spraying, plowing, and composting
4. Prevention, containment, eradication, and monitoring
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. The four main removal categories are manual (hand-pulling, digging, cutting), mechanical (mowing, brush cutting, forestry mowing, prescribed fire), chemical (herbicide applications like cut-stump, basal bark, and foliar spray), and biological (biocontrol insects, targeted grazing). Most effective management plans combine two or more methods — a principle called integrated pest management.

#### 4. When is the optimal time to hand-pull Garlic Mustard, and why?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. In fall after the first frost, when roots are weakened
2. In midsummer when the plant is tallest and easiest to find
3. In spring (April-May) before seed pods mature, to prevent adding seeds to the soil seed bank
4. In winter when the rosettes are visible against the snow
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **C**. Garlic Mustard should be pulled in spring before the second-year plants set seed, typically April through May. If plants produce seeds before removal, you have added to the seed bank rather than reducing it. Pulled plants should be bagged and removed from the site because Garlic Mustard can continue to ripen seeds even after being uprooted. Consistent annual pulling for four to six years is needed to exhaust the seed supply.

#### 5. What is the cut-stump herbicide treatment method, and why is it considered one of the most effective approaches for woody invaders?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Spraying herbicide on the leaves of a standing plant to kill it over several weeks
2. Cutting a woody plant at ground level and immediately applying herbicide to the freshly cut stump, which is highly targeted and prevents resprouting
3. Removing the bark in a ring around the trunk to starve the plant
4. Injecting herbicide into the soil around the root zone
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Cut-stump treatment involves cutting a woody plant at ground level and immediately applying herbicide (typically triclopyr or glyphosate) to the freshly cut stump surface. It is highly effective because the herbicide is transported directly to the roots through the plant's vascular system. It is also very targeted — only the treated plant is affected, minimizing damage to nearby native plants. The herbicide must be applied immediately because the cut surface begins to seal within hours.

    **Concept Tested:** Chemical Control Methods

#### 6. What Minnesota success story demonstrates the effectiveness of biological control for invasive species?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Native bees were released to out-compete Spotted Knapweed for resources
2. Galerucella beetles were introduced to control Purple Loosestrife and have dramatically reduced its populations
3. Goats were used to completely eradicate all buckthorn from state forests
4. A native fungus was discovered that kills Reed Canary Grass
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Galerucella leaf-feeding beetles from Purple Loosestrife's native range in Europe were tested extensively and then released in Minnesota wetlands. After years of careful introduction, these beetles have dramatically reduced Purple Loosestrife populations across the state, allowing native wetland plants to recover. This classical biological control success required years of research and regulatory approval to ensure the beetles would not harm native species.

    **Concept Tested:** Biological Control Methods

#### 7. What does Integrated Pest Management (IPM) mean in the context of invasive species control?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Using only organic methods and never applying chemicals
2. Combining multiple control methods (manual, mechanical, chemical, biological) into a coordinated strategy tailored to the site and species
3. Hiring a professional pest control company to manage the problem
4. Applying the strongest available herbicide to eliminate all vegetation and starting over
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Integrated Pest Management combines multiple control methods into a coordinated strategy tailored to the specific site and species. A typical IPM plan for a buckthorn-infested woodland might include forestry mowing to remove bulk plants, cut-stump herbicide treatment to prevent regrowth, hand-pulling of seedlings over subsequent years, and seeding native species to fill gaps. The IPM cycle includes assessment, goal-setting, implementation, monitoring, and adaptive management.

    **Concept Tested:** Integrated Pest Management

#### 8. A landowner clears a half-acre of buckthorn from their woodland and does not replant native species. What is the most likely outcome?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Native plants will quickly fill in the area within one growing season
2. The area will remain bare and weed-free for several years
3. Buckthorn seedlings from the soil seed bank and other invasive species will rapidly recolonize the disturbed area
4. Wildlife will bring in native seeds and restore the area naturally
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **C**. Removing buckthorn creates a disturbed area with increased sunlight and exposed soil — ideal conditions for buckthorn seeds to germinate from the soil seed bank and for other invasive species to colonize. Without planting native species to compete for light and resources, the site will likely become reinvaded. Establishing dense native cover after removal is a critical step that fills the ecological gaps left by the removed buckthorn.

#### 9. What is the single most important timing rule for invasive species removal?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Always remove invasive species in winter when they are dormant
2. Remove invasive species before they set seed to avoid adding to the seed bank
3. Wait until the soil is completely dry to minimize root fragment dispersal
4. Always apply herbicide on the hottest day of summer for maximum absorption
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Removing invasive plants before they produce and disperse seeds is the most important timing principle. If a plant sets seed before removal, you have added to the soil seed bank rather than reducing it. This principle applies across all species: pull Garlic Mustard before May seed pods mature, cut Wild Parsnip before flowering, and remove Purple Loosestrife before late-summer seeds ripen. Soil moisture conditions and herbicide timing are also important but secondary to the seed-timing rule.

    **Concept Tested:** Removal Timing

#### 10. Why is follow-up monitoring considered essential rather than optional in invasive species management?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. It is required by law for all removal projects in Minnesota
2. Without monitoring, you cannot detect regrowth, new seedlings from the seed bank, or new invasive species colonizing the disturbed site
3. Monitoring is only needed when herbicides were used, to check for chemical residue
4. It provides exercise and outdoor recreation opportunities for volunteers
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Monitoring after removal is essential because treated plants may resprout from stumps or root fragments, seeds in the soil seed bank will germinate over multiple years, and new invasive species may move into the disturbed area. The recommended schedule is three to four site visits in the first year, at least two visits per year for years two through five, and annual checks for at least five years after the last invasive plant is found. Removal without monitoring is like planting a garden and never weeding again.

    **Concept Tested:** Follow-Up Monitoring
