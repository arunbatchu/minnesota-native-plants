    Quiz: Systems Thinking in Ecology

    Test your ability to apply systems thinking concepts to ecological scenarios, analyze feedback loops, and evaluate interconnections in Minnesota ecosystems.

    1. What are the three essential characteristics of a system?

    1. Size, color, and location
    2. Components (parts), interconnections (relationships between parts), and purpose or function (emergent behavior)
    3. Inputs, storage, and waste
    4. Beginning, middle, and end
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. A system has three essential characteristics: components (the individual parts, such as plants, animals, soil, water), interconnections (the relationships between those parts, such as pollination, predation, and nutrient exchange), and purpose or function (the overall behavior that emerges from the interactions, such as a self-sustaining prairie). Understanding systems means focusing on the relationships between parts, not just studying each part in isolation.

    Concept Tested: What Is Systems Thinking?

    2. What is the difference between a negative feedback loop and a positive feedback loop in ecology?

    1. Negative loops are harmful; positive loops are beneficial
    2. Negative loops stabilize systems by counteracting change; positive loops amplify change and can lead to runaway effects
    3. Negative loops only occur in prairies; positive loops only occur in forests
    4. There is no meaningful difference between the two types
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. Negative feedback loops stabilize systems by counteracting change — like predator-prey cycles that keep both populations in balance. Positive feedback loops amplify change — like soil erosion: removing vegetation exposes soil, which erodes, which means fewer plants can grow, which exposes more soil. The names do not indicate good or bad — "negative" means counteracting and "positive" means amplifying. Both types are fundamental to how ecosystems behave.

    Concept Tested: Feedback Loops in Ecology

    3. A deer population increases dramatically after wolves are removed from a forest. The deer overbrowse native wildflowers and tree seedlings, causing plant diversity to collapse. What ecological concept does this illustrate?

    1. Edge effects
    2. Nutrient cycling
    3. A trophic cascade, where removing a top predator causes ripple effects through multiple levels of the food web
    4. Carbon sequestration
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is C. A trophic cascade occurs when changes at one level of the food web ripple through other levels. Removing wolves (top predators) allows deer (herbivores) to increase unchecked, leading to overgrazing that devastates plants (producers) and the insects and birds that depend on them. This concept demonstrates why systems thinking is essential — intervening at one point in an ecosystem can trigger unexpected consequences across multiple levels.

    Concept Tested: Trophic Cascades

    4. What are "emergent properties" in the context of ecosystems?

    1. Properties of individual organisms that only appear during spring
    2. Characteristics that arise from the interactions of components and cannot be predicted by studying individual parts alone
    3. New species that emerge after habitat restoration
    4. Chemical properties of soil that change with pH
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. Emergent properties are characteristics of a system that arise from the interactions of its components and cannot be predicted by studying individual parts in isolation. Ecosystem resilience, self-regulation, and biodiversity are emergent properties — they do not exist in any single species but emerge from the web of relationships among species. This is why a pile of individual prairie plants is not a functioning prairie; the emergent properties only appear when the system is connected and interacting.

    Concept Tested: Emergent Properties

    5. How do mycorrhizal networks function as a systems-level phenomenon in forests?

    1. They are purely parasitic fungi that drain energy from tree roots
    2. They form underground networks connecting multiple trees, facilitating nutrient and chemical signal exchange between individuals across the forest
    3. They only grow in disturbed soils after logging operations
    4. They produce visible mushrooms that are the primary food for forest animals
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. Mycorrhizal networks are underground fungal networks that connect the roots of multiple trees and plants, facilitating the exchange of nutrients (especially phosphorus and nitrogen), water, and chemical defense signals. Through these networks, mature trees can transfer resources to seedlings growing in shade, and trees under insect attack can send chemical warnings to neighboring trees. This "wood wide web" is a systems-level phenomenon that cannot be understood by studying individual trees alone.

    Concept Tested: Mycorrhizal Networks

    6. A city drains a wetland to build a shopping center. Two years later, neighborhoods downstream experience severe flooding that never occurred before. Apply systems thinking to explain why.

    1. The flooding is unrelated to the wetland drainage and must be caused by climate change alone
    2. The wetland was storing and slowly releasing floodwater; removing it eliminated that capacity, so stormwater now flows downstream faster and in greater volume
    3. The shopping center's parking lot is attracting more rain
    4. Drainage pipes from the development are pumping water into the downstream area
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. From a systems perspective, the wetland was performing a critical flood storage function — absorbing stormwater and releasing it slowly over time. Removing this component disrupted the hydrological system. The water that the wetland would have absorbed now flows directly downstream, arriving faster and in greater volume, causing flooding in areas that were previously protected. This is a classic example of unintended consequences from intervening in a system without understanding its interconnections.

    Concept Tested: Unintended Consequences

    7. Why does higher biodiversity generally lead to greater ecosystem resilience?

    1. More species means more competition, which makes each species stronger
    2. Greater biodiversity provides functional redundancy — multiple species can fill similar roles, so if one declines, others maintain the ecosystem's functions
    3. Biodiversity only matters for visual variety, not for ecological function
    4. High biodiversity always prevents all invasive species from establishing
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. Greater biodiversity provides functional redundancy — when multiple species fill similar ecological roles (pollination, nutrient cycling, predation), the loss of any single species does not collapse that function. A prairie with 200 plant species can lose several to drought and still provide pollinator resources because other species fill the gap. A monoculture lawn, with essentially one species, has no backup. This relationship between biodiversity and stability is one of the most important principles in ecology.

    Concept Tested: Biodiversity and Stability

    8. What is a keystone species, and how does the concept relate to systems thinking?

    1. The most common species in an ecosystem
    2. A species whose influence on the ecosystem is disproportionately large relative to its abundance — removing it causes cascading changes throughout the system
    3. Any endangered species listed by the federal government
    4. The largest species in a given habitat
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. A keystone species has a disproportionately large influence on its ecosystem relative to its abundance. Removing it causes cascading changes throughout the system, similar to removing a keystone from an arch, which causes the entire structure to collapse. In Minnesota, examples include beavers (whose dams create wetland habitat) and native oaks (which support more caterpillar species than any other tree genus). The keystone concept illustrates that not all system components are equal — some are critical connectors.

    Concept Tested: Keystone Species

    9. Evaluate this claim: "Planting a single row of native wildflowers along a farm field edge is sufficient to replace the ecological services of the wetland that was drained nearby." Is this claim valid?

    1. Yes, any native planting replaces the functions of any lost habitat
    2. No, a single row of wildflowers cannot replicate the flood storage, water filtration, groundwater recharge, carbon storage, and biodiversity support functions of a wetland — these are system-level properties that require specific conditions
    3. Yes, because wildflowers filter water the same way wetland plants do
    4. The claim cannot be evaluated without knowing the wildflower species used
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. This claim fails under systems analysis. A wetland performs multiple interconnected functions — flood storage, water filtration, groundwater recharge, carbon sequestration, and biodiversity support — that emerge from specific conditions (hydrology, soil saturation, specialized plant communities). A row of wildflowers provides some pollinator habitat but cannot replicate the hydrological, chemical, and biological processes of a wetland. Ecosystem services are system-level properties tied to specific structures and conditions, not interchangeable between habitat types.

    Concept Tested: Ecosystem Services

    10. Design a simple systems map showing how the removal of native prairie plants from a landscape could affect at least four different ecosystem components through interconnected pathways.

    1. Prairie removal only affects the plants themselves and nothing else
    2. Prairie removal reduces pollinator food, increases soil erosion, decreases carbon storage, and degrades water quality — each change amplifies the others through interconnected feedback
    3. Prairie removal benefits the ecosystem by creating open space for new species
    4. The effects of prairie removal are unpredictable and cannot be mapped
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. A systems map of prairie removal shows multiple interconnected pathways: removing prairie plants eliminates pollinator food sources (threatening bee and butterfly populations), exposes soil to erosion (degrading topsoil built over millennia), releases stored carbon from roots and soil (contributing to atmospheric CO2), and removes the root-based water filtration system (increasing nutrient runoff into lakes and streams). These effects reinforce each other — eroded soil supports fewer plants, which exposes more soil, creating a positive feedback loop of degradation. This exercise demonstrates why systems thinking requires tracing multiple pathways rather than examining single cause-and-effect chains.

    Concept Tested: Systems Maps in Ecology
