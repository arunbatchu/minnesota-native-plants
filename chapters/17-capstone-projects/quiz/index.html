    Quiz: Capstone Projects

    Test your ability to synthesize knowledge from across the course and apply it to real-world native plant project planning.

    1. The Garden Design Capstone project requires six phases. What is the correct sequence?

    1. Plant selection, site assessment, budget, design drawing, presentation, maintenance
    2. Site assessment, design goals and plant selection, design drawing, sourcing and budget, maintenance plan, presentation
    3. Presentation, design drawing, site assessment, plant selection, maintenance, budget
    4. Budget, sourcing, planting, maintenance, monitoring, presentation
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. The Garden Design Capstone follows six phases in order: (1) Site Assessment — evaluating sunlight, soil, moisture, and microclimates; (2) Design Goals and Plant Selection — choosing species matched to conditions; (3) Design Drawing — creating a layout; (4) Sourcing and Budget — finding plants and estimating costs; (5) Maintenance Plan — planning ongoing care; (6) Presentation — communicating your design to others. This sequence ensures that every decision is grounded in site reality rather than aesthetic preference alone.

    Concept Tested: Garden Design Capstone

    2. Which chapters of the course are most relevant to the Invasive Removal Plan capstone project?

    1. Chapters 1 and 2 only (introductory concepts)
    2. Chapters 8, 9, and 12 (invasive species identification, removal methods, and restoration)
    3. Chapters 13 and 14 only (cultural uses and resources)
    4. Chapter 17 only (capstone instructions)
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. The Invasive Removal Plan capstone draws primarily on Chapter 8 (identifying invasive species in the field), Chapter 9 (removal strategies, integrated pest management, timing, and follow-up monitoring), and Chapter 12 (restoring native plant communities after invasive removal). It also draws on Chapter 7 (plant identification skills) and Chapter 15 (systems thinking to understand cascading effects). Successful invasive management requires integrating knowledge from identification through restoration.

    Concept Tested: Invasive Removal Plan

    3. You are working on the Pollinator Corridor Plan capstone. What ecological concept must you apply to connect isolated habitat patches across a landscape?

    1. Carbon sequestration
    2. Trophic cascade dynamics
    3. Habitat connectivity — creating continuous or stepping-stone plantings that allow pollinators to move between isolated habitat patches
    4. Soil pH balancing across the corridor
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is C. A pollinator corridor plan requires applying the concept of habitat connectivity — creating continuous or stepping-stone plantings that allow pollinators (especially bees with limited flight ranges and migrating butterflies) to move between isolated habitat patches. This draws on knowledge of pollinator habitat needs (Chapter 6), native plant selection (Chapters 3-5), garden design principles (Chapter 10), and systems thinking about landscape-level connections (Chapter 15). The corridor must provide food, nesting, and overwintering resources along its entire length.

    Concept Tested: Pollinator Corridor Plan

    4. For the Field Guide Creation capstone, what combination of identification features should each plant entry include to be most useful in the field?

    1. Only the scientific name and the county where it was first discovered
    2. Common and scientific names, photographs of multiple features (whole plant, leaf, flower, fruit), bloom season, habitat, and key distinguishing characteristics
    3. Only flower color and approximate height
    4. A DNA barcode and microscope image of cellular structure
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. An effective field guide entry should include common and scientific names, multiple photographs showing the whole plant, close-ups of leaves, flowers, and fruits/seeds, bloom season and habitat information, and key distinguishing characteristics that separate it from similar-looking species. This approach applies identification skills from Chapter 7 and creates a practical tool that uses multiple overlapping features rather than relying on any single characteristic. Including both native species and their invasive look-alikes adds significant practical value.

    Concept Tested: Field Guide Creation

    5. The Community Education Presentation capstone asks you to communicate native plant concepts to a general public audience. What chapter's principles are most relevant for ensuring your information is accurate and well-supported?

    1. Chapter 3 (prairie plants)
    2. Chapter 16 (critical thinking and evidence-based practices)
    3. Chapter 4 (woodland plants)
    4. Chapter 13 (cultural uses)
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. Chapter 16 (Critical Thinking and Misinformation Detection) provides the framework for ensuring that your community presentation is grounded in evidence-based practices, uses credible sources, avoids common misconceptions, and presents claims that are supported by peer-reviewed research. When communicating with the public, it is your responsibility to check your own sources, distinguish marketing claims from science, and present accurate information — applying the same critical thinking standards you would use to evaluate others' claims.

    Concept Tested: Community Education Presentation

    6. You are developing a Systems Map Project. Which of the following would best demonstrate systems thinking rather than linear thinking?

    1. A timeline showing when each prairie plant species blooms
    2. A list of all native plant species found in your county
    3. A diagram showing feedback loops, interconnections, and cascading effects between plants, pollinators, soil microbes, water systems, and wildlife in a specific ecosystem
    4. A bar chart comparing the heights of ten native grass species
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is C. A systems map should show the web of relationships within an ecosystem, including feedback loops (both stabilizing and amplifying), interconnections between components (plants, pollinators, soil organisms, water, wildlife), and cascading effects that demonstrate how changes in one component ripple through others. Timelines, species lists, and bar charts organize information linearly but do not capture the circular, interconnected nature of ecological systems that is the core of systems thinking.

    Concept Tested: Systems Map Project

    7. The Maintenance Calendar Project requires you to create a month-by-month maintenance plan for a native planting. What is the most important principle to apply from Chapter 11?

    1. Native plantings need daily watering throughout the year
    2. Maintenance tasks must be timed to the plant community's seasonal needs, such as mowing before spring growth, avoiding disturbance during nesting season, and conducting prescribed burns in appropriate windows
    3. All maintenance should be concentrated in a single weekend in April
    4. Native plantings never require any maintenance activity
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. An effective maintenance calendar aligns tasks with seasonal ecological needs: spring weed management before natives emerge, avoiding mowing during grassland bird nesting season (May-July), timing invasive removal before seed set, conducting prescribed burns during appropriate weather windows, leaving standing plant material through winter for overwintering pollinators and bird food, and dividing perennials in spring or fall. The calendar demonstrates understanding that maintenance is an ongoing, seasonally-driven stewardship commitment.

    Concept Tested: Maintenance Calendar Project

    8. For the Resource Guide Compilation capstone, you need to organize Minnesota native plant resources from broadest to most local level. What is the correct organizational hierarchy?

    1. City, county, state, federal
    2. Federal, state, county, city and neighborhood
    3. State programs and laws, county-level programs (SWCDs), city-level programs and ordinances, educational institutions and nonprofits
    4. Alphabetical order by organization name
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is C. Chapter 14 organizes resources from broadest to most local: state-level programs and laws (MN DNR, BWSR, Buffer Law, grant programs), county-level programs (Soil and Water Conservation Districts, watershed districts), city-level programs and ordinances (native plant ordinances, community gardens), and educational institutions and nonprofits (University of Minnesota Extension, Minnesota Native Plant Society). This hierarchy helps users find the right starting point — typically their county SWCD for practical assistance.

    Concept Tested: Resource Guide Compilation

    9. Evaluate this capstone project approach: A student selects a garden design site, skips the site assessment, chooses plants based entirely on flower color preferences, and creates a visually appealing design drawing. What fundamental problems does this approach have?

    1. No problems; visual appeal is the primary goal of any garden design
    2. The approach skips site assessment, so plants may not match actual sunlight, soil, and moisture conditions, leading to plant failures regardless of how attractive the design looks on paper
    3. The only problem is that the student should have included more grass species
    4. Flower color is the only criterion that matters for ecological function
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. Skipping site assessment is the most fundamental error in garden design. Without knowing the site's actual sunlight levels, soil type, moisture conditions, and microclimates, plant selections are essentially random guesses. A sun-loving prairie plant placed in deep shade will fail, and a wetland species in dry sand will die, regardless of how beautiful the design drawing looks. The "right plant, right place" principle from Chapter 10 requires that site conditions drive plant selection, not aesthetic preferences alone.

    Concept Tested: Garden Design Capstone

    10. Imagine you are presenting your capstone project to a community group that is skeptical about native plant gardening. How would you apply principles from both Chapter 15 (systems thinking) and Chapter 16 (critical thinking) to make a persuasive, evidence-based case?

    1. Use emotional appeals and personal anecdotes only, since scientific data bores audiences
    2. Use systems thinking to show the interconnected benefits (pollinator support, water filtration, flood reduction, carbon storage), and support each claim with evidence from peer-reviewed sources or trusted agencies like MN DNR and University of Minnesota Extension
    3. Avoid mentioning any scientific evidence to keep the presentation simple
    4. Tell the audience they are wrong to be skeptical and should just trust your expertise
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. An effective, persuasive presentation combines systems thinking with evidence-based communication. Systems thinking demonstrates that native plantings provide interconnected benefits — supporting pollinators, filtering water, reducing flooding, storing carbon, and supporting wildlife — rather than just one isolated advantage. Critical thinking principles require supporting each claim with evidence from credible sources (peer-reviewed research, MN DNR publications, University of Minnesota Extension), acknowledging trade-offs honestly, and addressing common misconceptions directly rather than dismissing skepticism.

    Concept Tested: Community Education Presentation
