# Front-Yard Oak Savanna Edge — Planting Plan

**Site:** 8547 Crane Dance Trail, Eden Prairie MN 55344
**Lot:** 0.36 ac · Parcel 1311622340067 (Hennepin County)
**Exposure:** South-facing, full sun (>6 hr), stucco wall reflects heat → effective Zone 5a microclimate
**Pre-settlement vegetation:** Big Woods / prairie-forest border subsection — bur oak savanna on rolling moraine. This design restores the historic plant community of this land.
**Soil:** Likely Hayden, Lester, or Kilkenny loam (Hennepin moraine) — confirm via [USDA Web Soil Survey](https://websoilsurvey.nrcs.usda.gov/app/) before planting

## Design summary

A small pocket of Minnesota savanna anchored by a Serviceberry on the west end (echoing the existing entry crabapple's spring bloom), Ninebark shrubs flanking the bay window kept low for the indoor sightline, and a Gray Dogwood tucked behind the Serviceberry. The midground is drifts of native prairie forbs in warm savanna tones, connected by Little Bluestem and Prairie Dropseed grasses. A meandering limestone path enters from the existing concrete walkway on the east, curves across the front of the bed, and arrives at a partially-shaded bench pocket in the west third.

The existing river-rock drainage channel becomes a fully-vegetated bioswale (Rima's preference: no rock) — sedges and rushes form a fibrous root mat that armors the flow path while looking like a meadow.

See `annotated/01-front-design-overlay.jpg` for the visual plan.

## Bed dimensions

- Existing mugo bed: ~28 ft × 7 ft = ~196 sq ft
- New bed: curve forward into the lawn, deepest in the **west third** (~12 ft) where the bench pocket lives, tapering to ~7 ft at the central bay-window section, then meeting the existing arborvitae cluster at the east end
- Total new bed: ~360 sq ft (~80% increase)
- Mowing strip / soft edge between bed and lawn

## Phase 1 — Removal & site prep

Before any planting:

1. **Remove the mugo pines** (5-7 mature shrubs) — mini-excavator will be needed; their root systems are deep and stubborn. Loppers and a shovel will not be enough.
2. **Hand-pull the creeping charlie / ground ivy** in the river-rock area before disturbing soil. Disturbing it without removal first spreads it aggressively.
3. **Remove the river rock and any underlayment fabric** in the swale area.
4. **Test pit** in the swale area: confirm what's beneath — drain tile, gravel base, or just soil. Preserve any existing drain tile.
5. **Light re-grading** to ensure the swale still gravities flow toward the lawn / away from the foundation. Maintain 10 ft setback from foundation for the wettest swale zone.
6. **Soil amendment** if Web Soil Survey confirms Hayden / Lester / Kilkenny loam: minimal — savanna and prairie species prefer un-amended native soil. Do *not* add topsoil or compost; that fuels weed competition.
7. **Mark out new bed footprint** with a garden hose; let it sit a day; refine the curve to taste.

## Hardscape & seating

- **Path:** natural-cut Chilton limestone steppers, 18-24" wide. ~14 stones spaced for natural human stride. Set in screenings, not mortar.
- **Bench:** weathered cedar (Forever Redwood, Ash & Olive, or local maker) or natural fieldstone slab. ~5 ft long.
  - Orientation: **east-southeast** — sit *with* the savanna behind you, view *out* across the bloom toward the entry crabapple and cul-de-sac
  - Pad: pea gravel set on compacted base, ~5 ft × 5 ft, framed with steel edging or reclaimed brick
- **Bed edge:** soft, no steel edging — let the prairie spill onto the lawn line. A subtle trench edge re-cut each spring keeps the line crisp without hardware.
- **Mulch:** shredded hardwood, 2-3" deep. **Do not mulch the swale channel center** — sedge plugs go directly into bare soil so they knit together fast. Mulch in flow paths floats.

## First-year care

- **Watering:** every 5-7 days for first 6 weeks; every 7-10 days through first growing season; only during drought after that. Native plants want to root deep — light frequent watering trains shallow roots.
- **Erosion-control blanket** (jute or coir, biodegradable) over the planted swale channel for the establishment season. Stake at edges. Sedges break through it as they grow.
- **No fertilizer.** Native prairie plants are adapted to lean soils; fertilizer fuels weeds and makes forbs flop.
- **Weed pull** at 2-week intervals through the first two summers — most-aggressive weeds will be creeping charlie regrowth, dandelion, crabgrass at edges.
- **Mark the path** with stakes through the first winter so plants don't get crushed by the snowplow turn radius from the driveway.

## Year-by-year evolution

- **Year 1:** Looks "planted" — visible bare soil between drifts, clear individual plants. Many forbs won't bloom heavily this year (root establishment).
- **Year 2:** Drifts begin to fill in. Most forbs bloom. Sedges close the swale channel.
- **Year 3:** "Established" look. Bluestem and Dropseed at full size. Serviceberry and Ninebark visibly larger. Pollinator population has discovered the garden.
- **Year 5+:** Self-managing meadow. Some species will self-seed where they're happy (Bergamot, Coneflower, Black-eyed Susan if added). Edit gently.

## Maintenance calendar

- **March (late):** Cut grasses and stems back to 6-8" with hedge trimmers. Leave seedheads up through winter for birds. Mulch top-up.
- **April:** Inspect for winter damage on woody plants. Light pruning of Ninebark to keep below sill.
- **May-Jun:** Weed; deep-water if dry.
- **Jul-Aug:** Enjoy. Deadhead Bergamot if you want a second flush.
- **Sep-Oct:** Let everything go to seed. Resist the urge to "tidy."
- **Nov:** Cut back tender perennials only if heavy rot risk. Leave grasses and seedheads standing.

## Risk register

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Storm-event scour through the swale before sedges root | Erosion-control blanket year 1; consider French drain belt-and-suspenders if homeowner has seen historical flooding |
| Creeping charlie reinfestation | Hand-pull pre-removal; spot-treat through year 1; dense planting closes the canopy by year 2 |
| Lupine establishment failure (sometimes finicky from container stock) | Order from Prairie Moon as plugs grown in MN provenance; or seed in fall |
| Serviceberry transplant shock | Install in fall (preferred) or early spring; deep-water through first summer; B&B not bare-root |
| Snow pile from driveway crushes path edge | Mark path with tall stakes through first winter; talk to plow service |
| HOA / neighbor concerns about "wild" look | The Oak Savanna Edge palette is the most curb-friendly native option. Crisp bed edge, shrub backbone, and continuous bloom keep it reading as "designed." |
