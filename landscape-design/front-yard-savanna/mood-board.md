# Mood Board — Front-Yard Oak Savanna Edge

Curated visual references (all freely viewable, most CC-licensed) so you can see what each design element looks like in real, mature Minnesota landscapes.

Browse these with Rima before signing the install contract — they set expectations far better than any rendering. The point is *not* "make it look exactly like these" — it's "absorb the vocabulary so you recognize the look as it grows in."

---

## 1. The whole-garden feel

Oak savanna isn't manicured-perennial-bed and isn't roadside-meadow. It's something between: structured woody anchors with soft, layered prairie underneath. Look for these references:

- **[The Prairie Garden at the Minnesota Landscape Arboretum (Chaska)](https://arb.umn.edu/visit-the-arboretum/explore-our-gardens-collections/prairie-garden)** — drive 25 minutes from Eden Prairie. Walk it before construction starts. This is the single best visit you can make.
- **[University of Minnesota Bee & Pollinator Research Lab — Bee Lab demonstration garden](https://beelab.umn.edu/)** — St. Paul campus; explicitly designed to teach pollinator-friendly residential design.
- **[Eloise Butler Wildflower Garden, Theodore Wirth Park, Minneapolis](https://www.minneapolisparks.org/parks_destinations/parks__lakes/theodore_wirth_regional_park/eloise_butler_wildflower_garden_and_bird_sanctuary/)** — oldest public wildflower garden in the U.S.; shows mature woodland-edge composition similar to what your west side will become.
- **[Lebanon Hills Regional Park oak savanna restoration (Eagan)](https://www.co.dakota.mn.us/parks/parksTrails/Lebanon)** — 20 minutes east, restored bur oak savanna; shows what the *historic* community of your land actually looks like.

## 8. The "seasonal restraint" reference

A real designer's worry with native gardens is that they look "messy" out of season. Counter-references:

- **[Prairie Restorations Inc. — portfolio gallery](https://www.prairieresto.com/portfolio)** — the firm I recommend for this install; their portfolio shows mature savanna projects in winter and early spring.
- **[Larry Weaner Landscape Associates — case studies](https://www.lweanerassociates.com/projects/)** — East Coast but the design language is identical; he literally wrote the book on this style ("Garden Revolution," 2016).

## What to look for in every reference

A useful exercise: for each photo, ask yourself —

1. **Backbone:** can I see the woody anchors structuring the composition?
2. **Layering:** is there a clear short-medium-tall progression?
3. **Seasonal interest:** what's holding the composition together when nothing's blooming?
4. **Edge:** how does the planting meet the lawn / hardscape? Crisp or soft?
5. **Density:** does it feel "full" without looking weedy?

Apply the same test to your own bed at year 3 — that's how to know it succeeded.

## What we're *not* doing (anti-references)

Worth flagging things people sometimes confuse with native design:

- **English perennial border** — symmetric, color-blocked, mostly cultivars. Beautiful but not what we're doing.
- **Prairie restoration** (no shrubs, all forbs/grass) — wilder than the savanna edge; correct for a 5-acre site, too austere for a residential frontage.
- **Pollinator garden as "list of pollinator plants"** — without structure (woody backbone, repeated drifts, edge), it reads as random and gets called "weedy."
- **Native plants in cedar mulch beds with separation between every plant** — sterile; the whole point is canopy-closure between drifts so weeds can't compete.

The Oak Savanna Edge approach threads the middle: structured *enough* to read as designed, wild *enough* to be ecologically real.
